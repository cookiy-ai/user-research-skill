# Research Method Selector

Use this table to choose the lightest method that can answer the question with enough confidence.

| Method | Best for | Typical sample | Signal type | Main risk |
|---|---|---:|---|---|
| User interviews | Understanding goals, behavior, and decision criteria | 5-8 | Deep qualitative | Over-indexing on what people say |
| Usability testing | Evaluating a flow or prototype | 5-8 | Behavioral + qualitative | Mistaking task friction for product strategy issues |
| Surveys | Quantifying attitudes or segment spread | 100+ | Broad quantitative | Weak questions create false confidence |
| Diary studies | Seeing behavior over time | 8-15 | Longitudinal qualitative | High participant drop-off |
| Support ticket review | Finding recurring operational pain | 50+ tickets | Qualitative at scale | Biased toward vocal/problem-heavy users |
| Product analytics review | Validating reach or severity | Event-dependent | Behavioral quantitative | Tells what, not why |

## Selection Heuristics

- If the team needs to understand motivation or workflow, start with interviews.
- If the team already has a design and needs to test task success, use usability testing.
- If the team has a hypothesis from qualitative work and needs scale, add a survey or analytics check.
- If the issue unfolds over days or weeks, interviews alone are usually insufficient.

## Failure Modes

- Using surveys before the team knows what to ask
- Treating five interviews as a market-size estimate
- Treating analytics as user intent without qualitative evidence
- Mixing validation and exploration in one overloaded study
